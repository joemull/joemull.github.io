Before High Ground

Joseph Muller

April 29, 2019

Topic

This piece of “speculative curation fiction” was written for SI 667: Foundations of Digital Curation at the University of Michigan School of Information. In it I explore a dystopian future in which archives and information resources are captive to neoliberalism in the United States, but Canada has strong public information access policies that give people control over their own data and easy access to open data.

Story

“This year, the Canadian government set the living standard minimum at $204,000. That’s a 3% increase from 2069. The living standard minimum is one of the numbers that Employment and Social Development uses to calculate basic income for everyone living in Canada....”

There was a knock on the door. Rival paused the news and went to open it. Jodi and Roger were right on time. Jodi was a researcher working on an oral history project funded by Library and Archives Canada, and Roger was her assistant. As a historian herself, Rival was more interested in how the US Oral History Project was managed and conducted than the average US refugee. She was curious about Jodi’s methods, but she was determined not to ask too many questions or derail the interview with “meta” concerns.

When they were settled with tea, Jodi began. “I know I said this last week during our prep meeting, but I am glad to have you in this project. We don’t have a lot of histories yet from people who were born during High Ground and lived through the early 2060s in the United States.”

“Well, that’s why I agreed to do it.” Rival had told a lot of Canadians about her experience during High Ground since she made it to Canada back in 2064. She was a hit with people she just met because Canadians’ experience of that time was so different. Everyone wanted to hear the horror stories, like when New York started to flood every July and August, and people started moving to Pittsburgh, Roanoke, Knoxville, or Lexington if they wanted to stay in the east, or Minneapolis, Denver, or Tucson if they wanted to go farther, but there was no comprehensive response to the rising sea levels, only private companies selling the complete escape package for those who could pay.

And everyone always knew Rival was a US refugee immediately. Her first name was a dead giveaway. Sometimes people were polite enough to ignore it, especially in professional circumstances. But if the moment was right, or if someone was just that kind of person, they’d ask, “So, ‘Rival’—are you from the US?”

If she felt like being arch about it, she’d launch: “Yes, my name, Rival, originates from the era in the history of the United States when neoliberalism finally closed its grip on the body politic. Not content to send their children to freemarket kindergartens to give them a head start with the principles of innovation and market design, parents began labeling their children with the roles they hoped they would adopt in the late capitalist economy. ‘Searcher’, ‘Exec’, ‘Start’, and ‘Investor’ became common nicknames and even legal names for the newest generation.” Launching like this was usually fun in the moment, but it also usually freaked the other person out enough that they wouldn’t ask anything else, and she’d have to resuscitate the conversation.

Jodi’s voice broke into her thoughts. “I should also reiterate that this could jeopardize your record with hosters, since we have open data. You’re aware of that?”

“Yeah, I’m aware, and I’m OK with it. I’m not going back.” The thought of trying to live in the US again and deal with hosters was a nightmare. Hosters were the services that you subscribed to in order to handle a range of other subscriptions on your behalf, at any of several price points. For example, if she had become a “silver guest” with the hoster Candle, which would have cost $9,200 a month in 2064, the year she made it out across the Michigan/Ontario border, she would have had public water in her tap (might contain PFAS, lead, or bithalimopridolium), natural-gas-generated electricity that cut off in the hot parts of summer, access to shared autonomous vehicles on 2040-era roads during non-peak hours, Google’s ad-driven search environment of sponsored (and in Rival’s humble opinion, completely biased and commercialized) information, and some medical clinics for non-emergency visits. For $24,600 per month, she could have been a “gold guest,” which would get her spring water in her tap, wind-generated electricity, priority boarding on private autonomous vehicles on the Upper private highway network, access to high quality information through the Vera linked data network (including from noncommercial creators), and admittance to BlueCross private hospitals. And then there were platinum guest levels, but that was for people like Elon Musk III.

All through her life, she’d had to deal with the three big hosters already having terabytes of information on her, starting with her grandparents’ DNA, fingerprints, facial composites, medical records, shopping histories, social media data, sexual preferences, pets, work histories, education levels, and social and political beliefs.

By comparison, the thing Jodi was talking about was almost insignificant. When Jodi said talking to her would “jeopardize her record with hosters,” she was referring to the social loyalty scores (SLS) that the big hosters—Life, Candle, and ProQuest—used to give discounts on access to their service environments. If you said good things about a hoster that influenced other people and ‘converted’ them into ‘guests’, your subscription cost would go down. If you said something bad about the hosters, what happened would depend on who you were. You’d either get a huge discount, if you had a high algorithmic social influence, to try to win you back, or your subscription cost would go up astronomically, if you were just a normal person that no one really paid attention to. Rival wasn’t a celebrity, so talking bad about hosters in a publicly accessible oral history recording would almost certainly raise her subscription cost, if she ever went back.

Jodi continued. “As we discussed last time, I’ll be using a brite named Roger to help record. With your permission, he’ll connect to your Maple Leaf to do set up the data link and the permissions parameters. As you know, you can change the openness of the data as long as you live and through your will until the year... Oh, what was it?”

“2192 in this case,” said Roger. Roger was about four feet tall, with humanoid limbs and understated facial features. One of his arms ended in a high-quality virtual reality capture point. “I’ll start the recording now,” Roger said.

The Maple Leaf was a public digital ID that every Canadian had and could control. Digital data that was created by digital service providers that Jodi used—purchases, photos, locations, etc.—was stored in this ID, not in the providers’ own databases. This meant Rival could leave a service provider if she wanted, and take the data with her, only letting each service provider access the data she wanted them to access.

“Right, 2192,” said Jodi. “So once the interview is over, you’ll be able to view it through your Leaf and block out anything that you don’t want to appear. You’ll have two weeks to do that, and then it will go public by default. There will also be suggested blocked-out parts on there to help you. During the interview, Roger may also ask some questions if I forget anything, since he’s much better at checklists than I am. Humans make mistakes, and all that. So, any questions for me or Roger, since I know I didn’t have him with me last week when we met?”

“No, not really. Fortunately I could get some questions answered just by watching a few of the histories you’ve done. Accessing things like that would have been a nightmare in the US.”

“Tell us about that.”

Rival leaned back, looked out the window, and searched in her mind for a story she could tell that would make sense. “Well, say you wanted to learn about the history of the city you live in, which is Denver, because your family fled New Orleans during High Ground. That happened to be my research project. You don’t actually know how to do research, because that isn’t taught in the schools in the US. But you have a brite that’s set to ‘silver’ or maybe just ‘open’ if you can’t pay. We had ‘open’ after my brother got sick, because of his medical bills. And it’s anything from open. But anyway, you ask your brite, ‘Tell me about Denver before High Ground.’

“We had a brite named Sam. Sam would say, ‘Do you want to hear from Casey Webb or Bennie Kearns?’ Those were both celebrities. Then you’d pick one, and Casey Webb would start telling you about all the companies that had got their start in Denver. You didn’t ask about companies, but you’ll get entrepreneur-this, and brand-story that.

“I was trying to find information about the camps at the beginning of High Ground, when it was mostly low-income people from New Orleans and the Houston-Galveston area coming to Denver in droves, and there was a housing shortage, so people started forming camps. Since the public school systems in a lot of states shuttered in the 2030s and private internet home schools became the norm, people hadn’t been vaccinated for the Mospi virus, and the public tap water in Colorado had iltophirocene and itsabadium in it, and they couldn’t affa ord private water, so a lot of people got sick and died—it was a terrible time.

“But I digress. I didn’t know that much about the camps when I was first starting my research. All my brite would tell me is about the brand story of this big developer named Picket Fence. My brite said it saved countless people and gave them homes, but I found out later that it defrauded them by having them sign exploitative mortgages for shoddy bungalows. But first I had to go through a long process of teaching myself enough to figure out that there were things called archives that had old films and photos in them. Once upon a time, I think maybe before 2040, the US had public archives that you could visit for free, just like Library and Archives Canada. But we didn’t have any of those in Denver, or I didn’t know of any, so I decided to see if I could find anything in private archives.

“I figured out from another underground historian that if you are willing to write stories for brands, you’ll get access to their private archives, and there may be things in there that tell you something about other things than just that company.

“But it was a nightmare, because all the metadata was oriented around the brand. The one I got access to was SalesForce, which was used by Picket Fence to get people from the camps to sign these predatory mortgages. The search engine would prioritize the data for people with a high influence algorithm, so you would never see the data for people who were hurt by the system unless you went through dozens of records. And when you did that, you were flagged by the system and surveilled.

“I eventually got enough data to start writing my history, but by that time they found me and penalized me with a hoster rate hike, so I had to give up my silver level, and that meant doing everything was time consuming and dangerous. I started losing weight, my relationships fell apart because I had to work all the time, and I had an untreated medical issue that I couldn’t afford to fix. I decided to leave Denver. It took me three months to make it to Michigan, and finally got to Ontario. I can’t believe I didn’t run sooner.”
